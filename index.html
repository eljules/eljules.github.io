The Mental Health Crisis

Over the past several years our country has been experiencing a rise in the mental health crisis. This has led to an increase of alcohol consumption, substance abuse, and suicide. It was observed that before the pandemic, “Deaths from suicide, drug overdose, and alcohol-related liver disease, eclipsed the combined mortality of all infectious diseases combined in the United States.” (Magoon et al., 2020) There are many reasons for this overall decline in mental health, but the most predominant reason is a general lack of education and skills which are needed to deal with anxiety and depression.

Because the skills needed to overcome depression and anxiety rely on a fair amount access to resources and education, the lower class—particularly minorities—tend to be impacted by this the most. One study noted that,


Of course, mental health, like physical health, does not exist in a vacuum. When people’s basic needs are met, they are healthier in mind and body… Being poor is bad for your health. This certainly holds true in New York City, where black and Latinx people are disproportionately vulnerable to the coronavirus. (Magoon et al., 2020)

Aside from the lower class, anxiety and depression also have been taking a toll on the older generation of Americans. These generations tend to experience more anxiety and depression due to a lack of human connection, failing health, and growing separation from their loved ones. This generally goes unnoticed and untreated in our society. As a result, suicide is relatively high in this demographic of Americans. A study on the implications of the current pandemic stated that, “In 2018, older adults accounted for nearly one out of five suicide deaths (9,102 out of 48,344) in the U.S.” (Panchal et al., 2020)

The amount of attention and action in regard to the mental health of these demographics was very limited before the rise of COVID-19. As a result, the issue has grown out of proportion and has begun to affect far more than just the aforementioned groups.

The rise of the COVID-19 pandemic, and policy trying to curb the extent of its effect, has proved to be the catalyst of anxiety and depression for many Americans. Government mandated lockdowns have caused many people to lose their jobs and have caused people in the older generation to be more isolated amongst other things. Varying information in regard to protecting the vulnerable population has also proved to be a great source of stress and anxiety for many people. All of this ironically increased the influence of the pandemic to greatly affect those who have not caught the virus. However, since most efforts have been made to stop the virus and treat those who have contracted it, little to no effort has been made to help the many who are vulnerable to anxiety and depression. As a result of attention not being brought to the issue of increased anxiety and depression in the population, people generally did not have the knowledge or skills to properly cope with it during the pandemic. One study group found that, “Many adults are also reporting specific negative impacts on their mental health and wellbeing, such as difficulty sleeping (36%) or eating (32%), increases in alcohol consumption or substance use (12%), and worsening chronic conditions (12%), due to worry and stress over the coronavirus.” (Panchal et al., 2020)

One of the main contributors to this problem an increase of social media use while people are socially isolating. When looking at impact of social media during this crisis, one study indicated that “Because of widespread dissemination of unverified or downright fake reports that confuse the public…millions of individuals are relying heavily on social media while sheltering in place. There is growing concern that this trend may result in increased risk of depressed mood, anxiety, PTSD, and other mental health problems” (Magoon et al., 2020) Because people are isolated from others, they rely on social media to know the general condition of society. There are many stories and articles about the pandemic, amongst other things, which can spread a dark and twisted depiction of reality. People who consistently come across these skewed depictions tend to experience more anxiety and depression the more often they come into contact with them.

Because the older generation is more vulnerable to the virus, current social distancing practices and policies tend to leave them more isolated. It was observed that a lot of current government lock down policies, “may limit [the older generation’s] interactions with caregivers and loved ones, which could lead to increased feelings of loneliness and anxiety, in addition to general feelings of uncertainty and fear due to the pandemic.” (Panchal et al., 2020) This increased effort to protect their effort can also prove to be detrimental to their health when taken to the extreme. Isolation along with fear of the virus turns into a perfect storm of stress and anxiety. This turns into deep depression because these people are isolated from resources to help them cope during these troubled times. This may even lead to suicide in some cases where these people go too long without being treated or followed up on.

“Public education will be an essential tool for conveying accurate information about the role of lifestyle for enhancing emotional and mental wellness.” (Magoon et al., 2020)

The greatest way we can help this issue is by educating people on lifestyle changes and strategies which help counteract the negative effects of anxiety and depression. Learning to overcoming depression and anxiety is not an easy task, but it is certainly possible when we empower the people with proper knowledge on how to do it. This can be best done when programs are organized to help educate the people. It was noted that “Carried out at a community level and coordinated by volunteers, such programs will achieve maximum buy-in and can probably be planned and implemented at little or no cost.” (Magoon et al., 2020) By mobilizing in communities to best educate each other on how to battle the mental repercussions of COVID-19, we can help many people be healthier and happier. It could even save lives.

“Since media reports can be emotionally disturbing, contact with pandemic-related news should be monitored and limited.” (Pfefferbaum & North, 2020)

Social media also plays a key role in reducing the effect of anxiety and depression. The groups which are assembled to educate the community should teach people how to effectively monitor and limit their social media use. By separating from these platforms, people tend to find greater ability to cope with the pandemic. A study claimed that, “Social media interventions have been proven effective for changing perceptions and behaviors on a large scale, and may become an important part of a coordinated national strategy for psychoeducation and enhancing resilience in the face of COVID-19.” (Magoon et al., 2020) Though people may not realize it, social media can have a more negative impact on their health and happiness. This is why it is essential that we incorporate education on the effects of social media during community trainings.

Though COVID-19 has exacerbated the mental health crisis in America. It is a problem that should be taken as seriously as the pandemic itself. If people continue to ignore this issue, it is projected that there will be up to an additional 75,000 deaths from suicide and addiction which stems from untreated anxiety and depression. (Magoon et al., 2020) This issue will be best treated as we form programs on the community level to educate people on how to properly treat themselves and others who are experiencing anxiety and depression.
